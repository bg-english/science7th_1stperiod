Ecosystems & Their Components

Indicator 1 · Vocabulary & Environment

🔍 What is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms (biotic factors) interacting with each other and with their non-living environment (abiotic factors) in a specific area. Ecosystems can be as large as an ocean or as small as a puddle.

From a biblical worldview, ecosystems reveal God's wisdom. In Genesis 1, God created living things "according to their kinds" and placed them in environments perfectly suited for them — showing purposeful design, not accident.

☀️ Abiotic vs. Biotic Factors

Abiotic factors are the non-living parts of an ecosystem that affect living things:

  • Sunlight — energy source for photosynthesis
  • Water — essential for all life processes
  • Temperature — determines which organisms can survive
  • Soil — provides nutrients and support for plants
  • Air (CO₂, O₂) — needed for respiration and photosynthesis

Biotic factors are the living parts:

  • Producers (plants, algae) — make their own food via photosynthesis
  • Consumers (animals) — eat other organisms
  • Decomposers (fungi, bacteria) — break down dead matter and recycle nutrients

🏞️ Types of Ecosystems

  • Terrestrial: Forest, Grassland, Desert, Tundra, Rainforest
  • Aquatic (freshwater): Rivers, Lakes, Ponds, Wetlands
  • Aquatic (marine): Ocean, Coral Reef, Estuary
  • Urban/Modified: Cities, Farms — shaped by humans

Food Chains & Food Webs

Indicator 2 · Interdependence & Conservation

🔗 Food Chain vs. Food Web

A food chain shows a single pathway of energy transfer from one organism to another. A food web shows ALL the interconnected food chains in an ecosystem — a more realistic picture of how energy flows.

Energy flows in ONE direction: Sun → Producers → Primary Consumers → Secondary Consumers → Tertiary Consumers → Decomposers

⚡ What Happens If a Species Disappears?

If one organism is removed from a food web, it causes a cascade effect:

  • Its prey population may explode (overpopulation)
  • Its predators may decline due to loss of food
  • Plant populations may be devastated by unchecked herbivores
  • The entire ecosystem can become unbalanced

🌿 This is why God's creation is designed with interdependence — every part matters. Just like the gourd mattered to Jonah, every organism matters in God's ecosystem.

Fast Fashion & Ecosystem Impact

Indicator 3 · Environmental Impact & Stewardship

🏭 What is Fast Fashion?

Fast fashion refers to the rapid mass production of cheap clothing, driven by constantly changing trends. Companies produce huge quantities of low-cost garments designed to be worn only a few times before being thrown away.

  • The fashion industry produces 10% of global carbon emissions
  • It is the second largest polluter of water worldwide
  • Around 85% of textiles end up in landfills or are burned each year
  • Producing one cotton T-shirt requires 2,700 liters of water

☠️ Environmental Consequences

  • Water pollution: Chemical dyes and finishing agents are dumped into rivers, poisoning aquatic ecosystems and affecting food chains
  • Microplastic pollution: Synthetic fabrics (polyester, nylon) release microplastics when washed — these enter waterways and are consumed by fish
  • Soil degradation: Pesticide-heavy cotton farming depletes soil nutrients and kills beneficial organisms
  • Air pollution: Fabric production and textile burning release greenhouse gases and toxic chemicals
  • Habitat destruction: Deforestation for raw materials destroys ecosystems and reduces biodiversity

✅ What Can We Do? (God's Stewards)

Deuteronomy 11 reminds us that God cares for the land. As His stewards, we should:

  • Buy fewer clothes and choose better quality over quantity
  • Donate or upcycle old clothing (like the class project!)
  • Support sustainable and ethical brands
  • Learn to repair clothes instead of throwing them away
  • Avoid synthetic fabrics when possible
  • Spread awareness about fast fashion's true cost
